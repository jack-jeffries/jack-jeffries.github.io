\section*{Lecture series}

\vfill


\begin{itemize}

\item \textsc{Federico Castillo:} \textbf{Lattice Point Counting via Commutative Algebra}

\

\noindent We will review the theory of lattice points in polyhedra from the perspective of commutative algebra. Using tools such as local cohomology, one can recover many fundamental results from discrete geometry, and in some cases obtain the only known proofs of certain statements. Our main goal will be to discuss what is currently known (and what remains conjectural) about linear inequalities satisfied by the delta-vector of a lattice polytope.
\vfill

\item  \textsc{Daniel Duarte:} \textbf{The module of derivations of graded Tjurina algebras}

\

\noindent In the classification theory of isolated hypersurface singularities, the celebrated Mather-Yau theorem states that the Tjurina algebra is a complete invariant of the hypersurface. This result motivated the study of the module of derivations of the Tjurina algebra, which is known as the Yau algebra. This series of lectures will be an introduction to several questions on the structure of the Yau algebra in the case of homogeneous hypersurfaces. We will also discuss higher-order versions of these algebras and some recent conjectures about them.

\vfill

\item \textsc{Elo\'isa Grifo:} \textbf{Homological methods in commutative algebra}

\

\noindent There is a long and fruitful tradition of applications of homological algebra techniques to commutative algebra, starting with the Auslander-Buchsbaum-Serre characterization of regular rings. In this lecture series, we will introduce some of those homological tools, with an eye towards classifying interesting classes of singularities.



\vfill


\item  \textsc{Karl Schwede:} \textbf{Singularities in different characteristics}

\

\noindent In this lecture series we will learn about singularities in
commutative algebra in three different settings.  We will learn about
singularities in characteristic $p > 0$ (defined by properties of the
Frobenius map), characteristic zero (defined by resolution of
singularities), and finally
in mixed characteristic (defined by big Cohen-Macaulay algebras and
perfectoid algebras).  A goal will be to understand the analogies as well
as the links between these different types of singularities.

\vfill

\end{itemize}

\section*{Complementary lectures}

\begin{itemize}

\vfill

\item \textsc{Lara Bossinger:} \textbf{A cluster of algebra, geometry and combinatorics}

\

\noindent  In this talk I will introduce cluster algebras: certain commutative algebras defined recursively by sets of algebraically independent elements called seeds, and a combinatorial rule creating a new seed from a given one, called mutation. The intricate combinatorial structure underlying this construction has revealed its importance in many different areas of maths over the last twenty years such as polyhedral geometry, tropical geometry, combinatorics, birational geometry, knot theory and particle physics. In this talk I will give the basics of the theory, due to Fomin and Zelevinsky, and also summarize the state of the art regarding applications in physics.

\vfill

\item  \textsc{Javier Carvajal Rojas:}  \textbf{Singularities of foliations in positive characteristic}

\

\noindent  I'll explain to what extent the theory of F-singularities extends to foliations in positive characteristic. This is done through the so-called purely inseparable Galois theory equating foliations to purely inseparable covers and a generalized theory of tame ramification. 

\vfill

\item  \textsc{Joaqu\'in Moraga:} \textbf{Mutation semigroup algebras}

\

\noindent  In this talk, we will introduce a special kind of algebra called mutation semigroup algebras (MSA). 
These algebras were introduced by Enwright, Francone, Spink and the speaker. 
These algebras are a common generalization of semigroup algebras, cluster algebras, and Laurent phenomenon algebras. 
We will explain some of the main properties of mutation semigroup algebras and their connection with the geometry of some projective varieties.

\vfill


\item \textsc{Lisa Seccia:} \textbf{F-singularities of geometrically vertex decomposable ideals}

\

\noindent IIn combinatorics, vertex decomposition is an inductive method for breaking down simplicial complexes into simpler pieces. Knutson, Miller, and Yong introduced an ideal-theoretic analogue of this procedure, known as geometric vertex decomposition (GVD). This is a special degeneration technique that allows one to recursively decompose an ideal into smaller, simpler pieces while preserving many of their geometric and algebraic properties. In this talk, I will give an introduction to geometric vertex decomposition and discuss some recent and ongoing results about how singularities behave under this procedure.
Part of this talk is based on a joint work with E. De Negri, E.Gorla, P. Klein, and J. Rajchgot.

\vfill

\end{itemize}
